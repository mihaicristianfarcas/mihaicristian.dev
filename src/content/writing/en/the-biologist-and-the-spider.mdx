---
title: 'The biologist and the spider'
description: 'A biologist stages an experiment with spiders to show where paradise has been hiding all along.'
date: 2024-09-29
---

> "If you were to give man paradise, but hide it behind repulsive creatures,
> he would freeze with fear and 'feed the frogs.'"

Or at least that is what happened recently following an experiment which I
now have the distinct pleasure of presenting in a few words. A renowned
biologist held a gathering of scholars, from the field and beyond, which in
the world of science represented a phenomenon never before seen, and which
was brought up many times in various discussions, being a favorite of
critics and ordinary people alike.

The experiment consisted in the presence of some spiders — namely small
spiders, popularly known as jumping spiders — within a small assembly
representing a room whose walls adjacent to the right corner nearest the
spectators had been removed. The participants, a small but diverse part of
the crowd of observers of the experiment, handed over a DNA sample from
which a version of themselves, at micrometric scale, roughly the size of the
spiders, was placed inside that assembly, in the presence of the arachnids.

The way the above-described experiment unfolded is fairly intuitive. Each
mini-version of the participants suffered a tragic end, though probably not
exactly the one you were thinking of: once in the presence of the little
creatures, the clones shrank even smaller with terror, incapable of moving
from the spot, some even suffering bouts of vomiting. Consequently, they
would decompose, becoming once more the material from which they had been
created — a semi-solid, viscous liquid. The spiders did not move either, no
more than usual. Moreover, they were not troubled by such happenings; some
advanced curiously toward the "corpse" and returned gently to their initial
places.

After a few attempts at confronting destiny, the room began to look like a
swamp of monsters, an inferno without escape for the poor lost soul, victim
of merciless fate. The next mini-participants were already gripped by terror,
and despair melted them instantly.

Slowly, after all the clones had been exposed to the experiment, the
biologist spoke:

"The results, as you yourselves can observe, are the same for every
participant. The conclusion is that the nature of us all, the way we
function and think, are not as different as we expected, and faced with a
situation similar to the one I have presented, we might all have the same
pleasant or unpleasant reactions." Here he paused, gazing silently into the
hall at some of the participants, visibly bored and dismissive. On their
faces one could read displeasure and the impression that they had been
fooled, that their precious time had been abused. Seeing this, our biologist
added the following: "Your confusion has the gift of pleasing me, even
delighting me — at least this way I have the chance to somewhat overturn
your prejudices and premature conclusions." He took a needle from the table,
pricked his finger, taking a drop of blood and introducing it into a small
device used to create a new mini-sacrifice — or so the observers believed,
full of themselves and convinced. He took the new little one and placed him
in the midst of the spiders, who formed a circle around him. "You will
probably say that I, in my turn, shall suffer the same fate as you, since it
is only a matter of our essence, so little different, as was demonstrated
just now. Please direct your attention toward my small self now — you may be
in for a surprise…"

The little biologist, having arrived among the gang of spiders, looked at
them cautiously, attentively, motionless. He did not feel comfortable, yet
he forced himself to take a step toward one of them, more curious than
frightened. As he tried to touch them, they did not flee, but transformed on
the spot into the semi-viscous liquid from which they were formed. All of
them, except a single one, decomposed in this way, leaving in the end the
real spider and the biologist's clone, seated face to face. The clone felt
visibly awkward, this time a little frightened, and the spider did not let
him out of its sight. To everyone's astonishment, the mini-participant drew
near and, in a manner hard to understand and even harder to describe, as if
through a single deep gaze alone, formed a kind of pact with the spider,
thus establishing between them a relationship of full cordial respect. In
the following moments, to the astonishment of all the observers, both the
spider and the mini-biologist began to run to and fro, creating, shaping,
gluing, and crafting a multitude of unconventional objects and furniture out
of a mixture of web and semi-solid, viscous liquid. In the end, the room
looked impeccable, each "tenant" having his own half, both fully satisfied
with their shared achievement.

"Do you see now, despite your earlier opinions, what the real outcome of our
experiment is?" he said calmly, with a warm smile. "It was never a matter of
a simulated total opposition, of a place of chaos and despair into which
fate unscrupulously shoved the poor lost man, victim of our cruel and
merciless experiment, cannon fodder meant to delight us with the inevitable
decomposition occasioned by terror. No, ladies and gentlemen, no! If there is
anything to be understood from this whole amalgam of events, it is one
single thing: Paradise, shown to man through a repulsive, even hostile
environment — only in appearance, of course — is rather capable of driving
him away, of sending him wandering and making him go round and round in the
same circle, searching anywhere else — anywhere but before his very eyes —
for the much-coveted Paradise! He does not realize that he is in the right
place at the right time, that he is in the very place he has always been
searching for without truly knowing what he wants. His vision of Paradise is
altered, created entirely by the aspirations, values, and cultural and
social principles of our modern age, fully enlightened by protective reason
and by the free, independent spirit. 'And what else, and what more! What are
you trying to tell us with this, biologist? Suddenly we are naive and
perhaps even thick-headed — stupid, that is — in your opinion? Of all
people, you should be the cleverest? Is that what your experiment is about —
your own vanity?' you might be asking yourselves inwardly. This is exactly
where I wished to arrive, without in any way daring, through my intention, to
offend any member of my audience.

I want you to know, from the very beginning of my final address, that I am
in no way different from you. This little biologist, my clone, could have
belonged to any one of you; not one of you was less qualified or lacked an
essential quality, an inborn talent. On the contrary, my esteemed friends, it
is something that is learned, that is acquired, and it is simpler than you
imagine. You need only be open toward the world around you and honest with
yourselves — that simple. I know it is easy to say, and that inwardly you
complete my words with 'hard to do!', but I assure you that all you need is
sincere desire, and the rest will follow of its own accord, if you know how
to open your eyes, your ears, and, above all, your heart.

Why do you think, ladies and gentlemen, that I chose a spider as
co-participant in our experiment? Unfortunately for their family, that of
the arachnids, people feel an utter repulsion toward them; they hate them,
disdain them and, worst of all, cannot understand them. They do not realize,
or cannot accept, that these creatures too, just like themselves, are part
of nature's great creation, sheltered by the same Earth we all share, on
whose surface each unfailingly fulfills its role, under the blessing of the
almighty and eternal Creation. Does it not then seem somewhat absurd to you
that some should disdain others? Why? On what grounds? Forgive me, my dear
ones, but if you too had been created in such a way as to see as they see,
to have their reflexes, their mobility, their intelligence, to feed on
flying insects and to spin webs on top of it all, you may rest assured you
would have been no better-looking! Let us be content that they carry this
burden, and that they do so with such diligence — otherwise who knows what
would have become of us and of all the rest of the flora and fauna present
on Earth.

The same can be said of any other living being, regardless of size, color,
kind, and other classifications which do nothing but highlight the
differences between us and them, ranking some above others, willingly or
not. We must nevertheless not forget the codependence of all living things,
which precisely in this context is presented and considered so
superficially.

Now that you know these facts, these incontestable truths, vital to my
argument, let us return to our colleagues in the experiment. Does it still
seem to you truly as strange, unnatural, incredible, and incomprehensible as
before? Quite the opposite! It is the most natural thing of all. These two
understood each other from a single glance, and even though the nature of
each is different, they managed to establish an order out of the
surrounding chaos, an organization adopted and delimited between them
through something that cannot be expressed in words without harming, from
the very first instance, its truthfulness and authenticity. They created for
themselves — not in Heaven or anywhere else you may be inclined to believe,
but here on Earth, in a half-room made from a shoebox — a small Paradise of
their coexistence, a place which, believe it or not, is exactly as
harmonious as things ought to be on a larger scale as well. This, being
merely an experiment, does not represent reality exactly, for the calling of
man, as of the spider, is to be free; nor should it be ignored that both are
predators — but under the present conditions, an intelligent man realizes
that this attribute of theirs is of no use to them.

In closing, I want to say that humanity, in all its splendor, ought to
follow the example of these two friends. Yes, even the spider's — I am
inclined to say especially his, being in all things more honest than all
men put together.

And so, perhaps you have now managed to understand, at least in large part
and sincerely, what my message is and what the purpose of this experiment,
this performance, has been.
