title: 'Traveling in my early 20s'
description: 'Go where curiosity takes you, close or far — time is the only thing we actually own.'

---

It’s liberating. It’s challenging. It’s all I think of, all year long, and
I’ve been fortunate enough to visit different parts of Europe quite a lot
lately: Romania (represent!), Austria, Switzerland, Italy, France, England,
Scotland, Spain, and the list goes on.

My main goal when traveling has always been to go somewhere and immerse
myself in the culture, absorbing as much as I can from the world around me,
so that in the end I can find my place in it. It’s true that in capitals and
other major cities, life seems very similar between countries; it’s the
lesser-known places, small towns, and little stories you encounter along the
way that give you that actual sense of immersion.

I can easily say that traveling is the most rewarding thing I do, and have
been doing, ever since I’ve been able to travel on my own terms. Pushing my
comfort zone to the max, learning how to speak with people (in English and
not — heads-up for France, better bring that conversation guide along), how
to survive on as little money a day as possible (it’s doable to travel cheap
too; Switzerland taught me this, even though it’s considered an expensive
destination), how to see and read the cultural heritage all around me and
recall what I read about it back home… again, the list goes on.

> Travel is fatal to prejudice, bigotry, and narrow-mindedness, and many of
> our people need it sorely on these accounts.
>
> — Mark Twain

Whenever you get the chance, get out of the house. Go where curiosity takes
you, close or far. It will change you as a person, and the chances of
regretting it, or wishing you’d done something else instead, are very slim.
Money comes and goes (_I repeat, you don’t have to break the bank abroad_),
so make the most out of your **time**, which is the **only thing we actually
own**, as Seneca reminds us.

P.S.: A few tips:

1. Plan and book as much as you can in advance (a few days to a few months
   ahead).
2. If you’re under 25, in Europe you can get a bunch of discounts on
   attractions (_most of them are free_, e.g. museums — don’t fall for traps
   online) and on transportation, not only through DiscoverEU, but based on
   the policies of the countries you visit.
3. Apartments/Airbnbs + groceries + packing sandwiches for the road is the
   cheapest you can go, and might even give you an eat-like-a-local feeling.
4. Google Maps is your best friend (thanks to all the local guides who posted
   their reviews!). Use it for eating out or grabbing coffee — after a while,
   you’ll be able to spot good, underrated, lesser-known spots easily.

P.P.S.: It’d be hard to recommend destinations, so I’ll just leave you with
this: like with music taste, philosophy, and art, what grabs your attention
is what you should explore. Bon voyage!
